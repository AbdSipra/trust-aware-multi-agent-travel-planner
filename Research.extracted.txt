Collaborative Problem Solving in Multi-Agent
Large Language Model Systems: A Survey of
Architectures, Benchmarks, and Challenges
Abdullah Sipra, Ibrahim Ansari, Saad Khan
i221392, i220586, i220499

Abstract—Large Language Models (LLMs) have enabled the
development of autonomous agents capable of performing com-
plex reasoning, planning, and tool-based problem solving. Recent
research has explored extending these capabilities through multi-
agent systems where multiple LLM-based agents collaborate to
solve tasks that exceed the capability of a single agent. These
systems aim to improve performance in areas such as decision
making, task planning, tool usage, and autonomous experimen-
tation by distributing responsibilities across specialized agents.
However, significant challenges remain in coordination efficiency,
task planning, benchmarking, tool creation, and system security.
This paper reviews recent research on collaborative problem
solving in multi-agent large language model systems. We analyze
recent studies including FINCON, Smurfs, AgentQuest, Trav-
elPlanner, TOOLMAKER, and other related work that explore
multi-agent architectures, evaluation frameworks, automated
workflows, and security challenges in LLM agent environments.
The literature review summarizes the contributions of these
studies, compares different approaches proposed for multi-agent
collaboration, and identifies current limitations and research
gaps. The analysis shows that while multi-agent LLM systems
demonstrate promising capabilities for solving complex tasks,
several challenges remain in coordination mechanisms, reliable
evaluation methods, and security of agent communication.
I. INTRODUCTION
Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly ad-
vanced the development of intelligent agents capable of per-
forming complex tasks such as reasoning, planning, and tool
usage. Recent research has explored extending these capa-
bilities through agent-based systems in which LLMs operate
as autonomous agents interacting with external environments
and tools. These agents can perform multi-step reasoning,
generate code, and assist in decision making across a variety
of domains.
Despite these advances, many complex tasks remain difficult
for a single LLM agent to solve effectively. Tasks such as long-
horizon planning, large-scale data analysis, and autonomous
experimentation often require multiple reasoning stages and
interactions with external tools. Previous studies have shown
that LLM agents still struggle to solve complex real-world
planning problems and autonomous data science workflowseffectively [1], [8]. Similarly, automated scientific discovery
systems face challenges in exploring large experimental spaces
and evaluating generated solutions [5].
To address these limitations, recent research has proposed
multi-agent architectures in which multiple LLM agents col-
laborate to solve complex tasks. In these systems, agents
may perform specialized roles such as planning, analysis, or
evaluation while coordinating with other agents to complete
tasks. For example, the FINCON framework introduces a
hierarchical multi-agent architecture consisting of manager
and analyst agents designed to support financial decision
making [7]. Similarly, the Smurfs system proposes a modular
multi-agent architecture to improve tool planning and reduce
inefficiencies observed in earlier planning approaches [6].
In addition to architectural innovations, researchers have
also proposed new frameworks for evaluating and analyzing
LLM agents. The AgentQuest framework provides a modular
benchmarking system that evaluates agent performance using
metrics such as progress rate and repetition rate rather than
relying only on final task success [3]. The TravelPlanner
benchmark further evaluates the ability of language agents
to solve complex real-world planning problems involving
multiple constraints [8].
While these studies demonstrate promising progress in col-
laborative LLM agents, several challenges remain. Multi-agent
systems may suffer from inefficient coordination, limitations
in tool creation, and vulnerabilities to adversarial attacks. For
example, recent research has shown that multimodal agent
systems may be vulnerable to large-scale adversarial attacks
capable of spreading across agent networks [9]. These findings
highlight the importance of further research into reliable and
secure collaborative LLM systems.
This paper reviews recent research on collaborative problem
solving in multi-agent large language model systems. The
objective of this study is to analyze current architectures,
evaluation frameworks, and application scenarios proposed in
recent literature. By comparing the contributions and limi-
tations of existing approaches, this paper aims to identify

II. LITERATUREREVIEW
Recent research on collaborative LLM agents explores sev-
eral directions including multi-agent architectures, evaluation
frameworks, automated tool creation, and security challenges
in agent systems.
One important research direction focuses on the develop-
ment of multi-agent architectures designed to improve coordi-
nation and task planning. The FINCON framework proposes
a hierarchical multi-agent architecture for financial decision
making in which manager agents coordinate the actions of
analyst agents [7]. The system incorporates a risk control
mechanism to address decision making in volatile financial
environments. Similarly, the Smurfs framework introduces a
modular multi-agent architecture designed to improve tool
planning efficiency by reducing redundant context usage and
addressing limitations of earlier planning methods such as
rollback instability and premature termination [6]. Both ap-
proaches demonstrate how dividing tasks among multiple
agents can improve problem solving compared to single-agent
systems.
Another line of research focuses on evaluating the perfor-
mance of LLM agents through benchmarking frameworks.
AgentQuest proposes a modular benchmarking system de-
signed to analyze the behavior of agent systems during task
execution rather than evaluating only final success outcomes
[3]. The framework introduces metrics such as progress rate
and repetition rate that help researchers identify where agent
systems fail during complex tasks. Similarly, the TravelPlanner
benchmark evaluates the ability of language agents to solve
real-world planning tasks involving multiple constraints such
as transportation, cost, and scheduling [8]. Experimental re-
sults show that current language agents still struggle to suc-
cessfully complete many complex planning tasks, highlighting
limitations in current agent capabilities.
In addition to planning and evaluation, researchers have
explored the role of tools and automated workflows in multi-
agent environments. The TOOLMAKER framework investi-
gates how LLM agents can autonomously transform research
papers containing code into usable tools, allowing agents to
extend their capabilities without manual tool implementation
[4]. The CodeScientist system further explores the potential of
agent systems in automated scientific discovery by combining
literature analysis with code-based experimentation to generate
and evaluate research ideas [5]. These studies demonstrate how
collaborative agent systems may support complex workflows
involving both reasoning and experimentation. Related work
on tool use also shows that decomposing agent functions
across multiple LLMs can improve tool learning compared
to relying on a single LLM alone [2].Security and reliability have also emerged as important
research concerns for multi-agent systems. The Agent Smith
study demonstrates that multimodal LLM agents may be
vulnerable to adversarial inputs capable of spreading across
large multi-agent networks through a phenomenon described
as infectious jailbreak attacks [9]. Complementing this work,
the survey titledAI Agents Under Threatidentifies several
security challenges affecting AI agent systems, including un-
predictable user inputs, complex internal execution processes,
and interactions with external environments [10]. These studies
highlight the potential risks associated with deploying large-
scale agent systems.
Benchmarking work also reveals that current agent capa-
bilities remain limited in demanding real-world environments.
DA-Code introduces a benchmark for agent-based data science
code generation and shows that current LLMs still struggle
to autonomously complete complex data science tasks [1].
This supports the broader conclusion that although multi-agent
collaboration is promising, existing agent systems continue to
face important limitations in planning, execution, and reliabil-
ity.
Although these studies demonstrate significant progress in
collaborative LLM agents, several challenges remain. Current
multi-agent systems still face limitations in coordination ef-
ficiency, reliable evaluation of collaborative reasoning, and
protection against adversarial attacks. In addition, benchmark-
ing frameworks continue to reveal weaknesses in planning
and tool usage capabilities of existing agents. These chal-
lenges suggest that future research should focus on improving
coordination mechanisms, developing more robust evaluation
methods, and addressing security vulnerabilities in multi-agent
environments.
III. CONCLUSION
Recent research shows that collaborative multi-agent LLM
systems provide a promising direction for solving complex
tasks that exceed the capability of single agents. Existing
studies demonstrate progress in hierarchical coordination, tool
planning, benchmarking, automated workflow generation, and
system evaluation. However, the literature also shows that
important challenges remain in coordination efficiency, ro-
bustness, security, and reliable evaluation. Future work should
focus on building multi-agent systems that are more scalable,
secure, and effective in real-world problem solving environ-
ments.
